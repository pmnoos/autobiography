Chapter 19

"A New Beginning: My Journey to Australia"

We arrived in Brisbane for the World Expo in the second week of April 1988. The Expo had just opened that month and was set to run until October. It was a great opportunity to find employment. With my roots in the UK, I applied to work at the English stand and was instantly hired as a Chef at the Britannia Inn—a replica of a traditional English pub.

The shifts were long and incredibly busy, but it was a lot of fun. The atmosphere was completely different from anything I had experienced before. The Expo provided a captive audience, and the crowds were there purely for enjoyment. Those of us working in the various country stands were there not just to serve but to enhance visitors’ experiences by representing our respective nations.

I worked at the Britannia Inn until the end of the Expo. The restaurant was operated by David Peters Restaurants, which had won the contract to run it. It was so successful that they purchased the replica pub, obtained the necessary permissions, and reopened it in Brisbane’s Hyatt Arcade in November. They offered me a chef’s position, which I gladly accepted.

At the Expo, the Britannia Pub had both an Executive Chef and a Head Chef. Each week, they would arrive dressed immaculately in freshly laundered uniforms, with their chef hats getting progressively taller. This went on for several months, each trying to outdo the other. Word spread across the Expo site, and it became a source of amusement for other restaurant teams, with their own chefs joining in the hat competition. It was hilarious to watch these self-important chefs parade around, showing off.

Ironically, neither the Executive Chef nor the Head Chef actually worked in the kitchen with me and the rest of the team. By the end of the first week at the newly opened Britannia in Brisbane, both were gone—the Executive Chef was sacked, and the Head Chef managed to chop off his thumb while demonstrating to apprentice chefs how to cut up a chicken!

Shortly after, I was offered the Head Chef position, which I accepted. The owners brought in a new Executive Chef, a Spanish gentleman. It was a strange setup—despite being Head Chef, I was never consulted on how the restaurant should operate or what direction it should take.

At one point, the restaurant introduced a new kitchen at the back, facing Elizabeth Street Mall. It had an Oriental design and served cuisine to match. They hired a Thai chef to run that section, meaning I had no involvement in it. My expertise at the time was in Italian, French, and English cuisine, so I didn’t know much about Oriental cooking.

Months passed, and the Britannia was doing big numbers. Everything seemed to be going well, but the Executive Chef became increasingly agitated. Eventually, he pulled me aside and asked me to defend him to the owners. I had no idea what was going on. I’ve never liked getting involved in workplace politics or drama, so I stayed out of it.

Just before I was offered the Head Chef position, I had been looking into a franchise opportunity with Silvio’s Pizzas…

Silvio’s Pizza: A Franchise Experience

Just before I was offered the Head Chef’s position, I had been exploring a business opportunity with Silvio’s Pizza, a franchise run by an Italian family. The company operated pizza delivery stores throughout Brisbane’s suburbs.

Like any business, I knew it was important to do my due diligence, so I took the franchise's financial records to an accountant. He was supposed to investigate the business thoroughly, and his conclusion? "Everything seems fine."

With that assurance, I struck a deal with the main figure running the business—one of the family members, whose name was Silvio.

Running the Stones Corner Store

The store I acquired was located in Stones Corner, a busy suburb close to Brisbane’s city center. It had been managed by one of Silvio’s appointed managers before I took over. To maintain consistency, I kept him on staff, along with several young men—mostly students—who worked as delivery drivers. We didn’t want to interfere with the flow of production and operations.

At first, the store seemed like a great investment. It was busy every night of the week, especially during the dinner period. But after five or six months, I began to notice that things weren’t quite as they seemed.

At the same time, another franchise store in Capalaba had been sold to another franchisee. Every two weeks, we attended franchise meetings at Silvio’s head office to discuss marketing strategies and promotional offers.

And that’s when we made a shocking discovery.

The Truth About Silvio’s Franchise Model

Locked Out of My Own Store

Every franchise meeting turned into a battle between us and Silvio’s management. Eventually, they’d had enough.

One day, both I and the Capalaba franchisee were locked out of our stores.

I didn’t fight it. To be honest, I was relieved to walk away.

My original deal had been that I’d place a small deposit and pay the rest after selling my business in New Zealand. Since I had kept postponing the payment, I hadn’t lost too much money in the process. But I can’t say what happened to the other franchisee in Capalaba.

As for Silvio’s? Their model was unsustainable, and they soon realized they couldn’t expand interstate under their own brand name.

That’s when an opportunity arose—Domino’s Pizza was up for sale.

Silvio’s acquired Domino’s, rebranded their stores, and began growing the business under the new name.

And here’s the twist—one of those young store managers from the Silvio’s days eventually bought out the company. Today, he is the CEO of Domino’s Queensland.
