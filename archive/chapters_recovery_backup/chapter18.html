Chapter 18

An Unstoppable Journey: A Life of Ambition, Resilience, and Constant Reinvention Across Countries and Careers

In the mid-seventies, as the children grew older, I developed an interest in taking them to different restaurants. This was partly for my benefit, as I still had a strong inclination toward hospitality. In the earlier days, as I believe I’ve mentioned in a previous chapter, we used to host full-on afternoon teas for family and friends. Taking the children to restaurants was, in part, a way for me to stay connected to the industry and keep up with trends. However, at that time, New Zealand had a limited selection of places to enjoy a good meal out.

  That said, a few restaurants were starting to change the scene. One such place was a restaurant called Scoffers Inn, owned and operated by an English couple, Cliff and Zoe, who had fantastic ideas about how to run a restaurant with great menus and elegant décor. This was their second restaurant—the first, Nosh Inn, had equally creative charm, even in its name.
  
  After visiting Scoffers Inn several times, we became friendly with Cliff and Zoe. It was during one of these visits that they mentioned they were planning to sell the restaurant for personal reasons. Despite owning Magner Knitwear and having five children to care for, I seriously considered purchasing the restaurant. However, in their wisdom, they decided not to sell it to me. That’s the kind of thoughtful people they were—they cared about my family and recognized the challenges we would face if we took on such a demanding business.
    
 Later, in the early eighties, while we were still operating Magner Knitwear, we reconnected with Cliff and Zoe after they opened another beautifully designed restaurant called The Sussex. Eventually, we agreed to run The Sussex on Thursdays, Fridays, and Saturdays for three months while they went overseas—a truly wonderful experience. Sheila took charge of making the desserts under Zoe’s guidance, Julia and Phillip helped in the kitchen, and I managed the front of the house.

You might think, "This guy has it made—he’s been in New Zealand for just 15 years, owns a six-bedroom home with a swimming pool, and has raised five children. All he has to do is sit back, find some part-time work, and enjoy life."

Well, think again.

Life had other plans for me.

After Magner Knitwear, I found myself on a new path, one that led through three more businesses and three more homes. Each step brought fresh challenges, unexpected turns, and—sometimes—outright chaos.

 

First, I bought a property in Redvale, north of Auckland, with a glasshouse for growing tomatoes.

Growing Glasshouse Tomatoes at Redvale

I never gave much thought to growing tomatoes—especially glasshouse tomatoes—until I found myself doing exactly that. I had no real experience in it, apart from what I learned from Henry, who had helped plant tomato plants in the garden back at Gordon Avenue.

The property had two working glasshouses, with a third one still under construction. Our plan was to get the two operational ones up and running for full tomato production, which would take up all our time. We had no intention of hiring help—it was going to be just us and the glasshouses.

At the time, the glasshouses were empty, as the previous owner had just completed his growing season. They needed to be cleaned out and fumigated before we could plant a new crop. Typically, you could harvest two crops a year, with the winter crop being the most rewarding.

Starting out, we had to be careful with plant selection, as choosing the right variety was crucial. We also had to ensure we had a reliable water supply and adequate heating for the winter months.

Looking back, when I first saw the property, I had been more focused on the house and location rather than the actual practicalities of growing tomatoes. In hindsight, I probably should have asked a lot more questions.

A Lesson in Plumbing—The Hard Way

While I was learning about tomatoes, I also learned a thing or two about water systems—the hard way.

At the time, we had no town water supply—we relied completely on rainwater collected from the roof and stored in a huge round water tank beside the house. This single tank held all the water we needed for the home.

One day, I noticed a small leak at the very bottom of the tank. Water was slowly dripping from a tap. Thinking I could fix it, I grabbed my brand-new red toolbox—which, at that point, hadn’t seen much use—and pulled out a shiny spanner.

Now, you can probably guess what happened next.

I gave the tap one small turn with my spanner—and boom! The tap blew right off! A huge deluge of water came rushing out, completely emptying the tank within 15–20 minutes.

All I could do was shout, “Someone get a plumber!”

Needless to say, I wasn’t the family’s favorite person that day. We had to wait several days for a water delivery, and in the meantime, everyone had to be extremely careful with whatever little water was left.

Life at Redvale

At the time, Paul and Kathryn were at university, Julia was working at a bank, Phillip was finishing his last year at Rosmini, and Carolyn was about to start at Carmel College.

Despite my plumbing disaster, we did get the glasshouses planted and managed to grow six or seven crops while we lived there. The final crop was the best one we had—it was even considered the top-quality produce at the Auckland fruit and vegetable market that year.

During our time at Redvale, we also extended the house and built a new farmhouse kitchen—a large, welcoming space where the whole family could gather for meals. The benches and cupboards were made of Fijian Kauri, and it truly became a wonderful place to cook, eat, and share family moments.

Moving On, But Not Forgetting

After the water tank disaster, it was suggested that—before I could do any more damage—I should look for a part-time job. So, I got a job as a knitting machine operator at a knitwear clothing company in Auckland.

Looking back, Redvale wasn’t just about growing tomatoes—it was about the memories, lessons, and family time we shared. There were so many events and experiences there that I could probably write an entire book just about Redvale.

For three years, we worked the land, nurturing crop after crop while adjusting to country life. It was hard work, but it had its moments. (I could write a whole chapter on the mishaps and adventures we had there.)

And may I will.

Then came The Kitchen, a new business, followed by a home on Manuka Road in Glenfield.

From Redvale to the Kitchen Business

Selling Redvale turned out to be quite an experience. The property had been on the market for some time, but in the end, there were two serious buyers. The real estate agent knew exactly how to work them, playing them against each other in a Dutch auction.

Fortunately, we ended up getting a good price for the property—even though we never finished the third glasshouse. Looking back, I believe it was the kitchen renovation that helped seal the deal.

A New Home in Glenfield

After selling Redvale, we found a new home in Glenfield, still on Auckland’s North Shore. It was a three-bedroom house with a great dining room, a lovely lounge, and something truly special—two giant Redwood trees. They reminded me of the ones in California, and the house had a large deck built around them.

The back garden was equally impressive, with a small stream running through it. Growing along the edges was fresh watercress, perfect for salads and sandwiches. The house itself sat on an elevated brick base, which provided a spacious downstairs area. It also came with a large detached garage.

We settled in quickly, but—as always—it wasn’t long before I started looking for something to do.

Starting Another Business

Looking back now, I realize I had plans for Manuka Road that I never followed through on. The property had half an acre of land, split by the stream, which would have been perfect for a large garden—flowers, vegetables, the works. But instead of focusing on that, I found myself on the hunt for another business.

I’ve never liked working for someone else.

That’s when I came across a small commercial kitchen for sale. It was in an old butcher’s corner shop, complete with a large bank of freezers, stainless steel benches, and a designated area for an oven and dishwashing. It was the perfect setup.

We started producing desserts, quiches, cheesecakes, cakes, and muffins, supplying them to delis, pubs, hospitals, and restaurants across Auckland City. I loved the creativity of it all—experimenting with recipes, making delicious products, and seeing them enjoyed by so many people.

Another One Bites the Dust

I can’t recall exactly how long we ran the business, but I do remember enjoying it.

Then, one day, someone walked in off the street, saw what we were doing, and loved it. They made an offer on the spot, and we accepted.

Just like that—another one bites the dust.

From there, I jumped into the restaurant industry with the purchase of a pizza restaurant, The El Greco , moving our home once again—this time to Onewa Road in Birkenhead.

El Greco – A Slice of Success

El Greco was a great little business. Our main trade was takeaway pastas and pizzas, with a small dining room that could accommodate a dozen or so patrons.

We bought the pizza shop from two Greek guys, who insisted—quite emphatically—that the Greeks, not the Italians, invented pizza. We didn’t argue with them. Their recipes and procedures were already well established, and to be fair, they knew what they were doing.

At the time, food delivery services didn’t exist. If people wanted a pizza, they had to call in their order and pick it up themselves.

Running the Business

Our workday started around 10 AM, prepping everything for the night ahead. Sheila and I would spend the mornings in the back kitchen, while we had a young man named Ken handling prep in the front of house.

Ken was a great lad—tall, handsome, with a head full of curly hair. At just nineteen or twenty, he was already a true professional when it came to making pizzas.

The pizza dough recipe was the heart of the business, and Ken had perfected the art of handling it. He taught me a lot—how to work the dough, shape it properly, and get the best results. During service, he’d toss the dough high into the air, much to the delight of our customers. It became part of the El Greco experience, turning a simple wait for food into entertainment.

We were even regularly visited by players from the New Zealand All Blacks, who came in for a good feed of carbohydrates before a game.

The menu was simple but well-loved. (See menu link.)

A New Home & A New Question

As the business grew, we decided to move closer to the shop. We found a modern three-bedroom townhouse, with city views, an integrated garage, and a master bedroom with an en suite—all spread over three levels.

At this point:

  • Paul and Kathryn were at university
  • Julia and Phillip had gone overseas
  • Phillip had just completed his Engineering degree
  • At home, it was just Sheila, Carolyn, and me

Business was going well. Life was good. I thought we were all happy.

Then, out of the blue, Sheila asked me:

"Have you ever thought about living in Australia?"

I was caught off guard. I had always talked about taking a holiday overseas, but moving? That was a different story.

"Where in Australia are you thinking?" I asked.

"Brisbane."

That surprised me even more.

"Brisbane? But you’ve never been there."

I knew Brisbane in the early 80s—it was hot, humid, and far from modern. It wasn’t like Sydney or Melbourne. It was still growing up as a city.

But now that the idea had been spoken aloud, it couldn’t be ignored.

And to my surprise—Carolyn was on Sheila’s side.

Looking back, I sometimes wonder: Was I searching for success? Or was I simply chasing something unknown?

And then, my journey took an even bigger leap—I moved to Australia.

Australia became a fresh start, an entirely new chapter filled with unexpected opportunities.
